Why I Chose to Transition Into IT After the Military

Introduction

Transitioning out of the military can be difficult, especially when trying to decide what career path to take next. After serving as an Army Intelligence Analyst and medically retiring, I found myself trying to figure out what direction I wanted my future to go. I knew I wanted a career with opportunities for growth, flexibility, and long-term stability. That is what led me to begin pursuing a career in Information Technology (IT).

At first, the IT field felt overwhelming because there are so many different career paths to choose from. Cybersecurity, networking, cloud computing, help desk support, and web development all seemed interesting in different ways. Even though I am still figuring out exactly where I want to specialize, I knew IT was a field worth exploring.

Transferable Skills from the Military

One of the biggest reasons I felt drawn to IT was because many of the skills I developed in the military transfer into the technology field. As an Intelligence Analyst, attention to detail was critical. Missing important information or making careless mistakes could have serious consequences. In IT, attention to detail is equally important, whether troubleshooting a technical problem, identifying cybersecurity threats, or configuring systems.

The military also taught me how to problem-solve under pressure. Things rarely go exactly as planned, and you often have to think critically and adapt quickly. This mindset applies directly to IT because technology constantly changes, and troubleshooting problems often requires patience and persistence.

Another skill that carries over is the ability to continuously learn. In the military, training never really stops, and that same mindset is necessary in IT. Technology evolves quickly, meaning professionals in the field must continue learning throughout their careers.

Challenges of Starting a Career in IT

Although I am excited about learning IT, I would be lying if I said the process has been easy. One of the biggest challenges has been the pace of certification training. I am currently enrolled in a program that covers multiple certifications in a short amount of time. Sometimes it feels like there is not enough time to absorb everything before moving on to the next topic.

Another challenge has been deciding which area of IT interests me most. Cybersecurity seems exciting because of the problem-solving and analytical side of the work. Networking and cloud technology also interest me because they play such a major role in how systems function. At times, having so many choices can feel overwhelming.

Despite the challenges, I remind myself that it is okay not to have every answer right away. Many people in IT start in one role and eventually discover what they enjoy most over time.

Why IT Is Worth It

Even with the challenges, I believe IT is worth pursuing because of the opportunities it provides. Technology careers continue to grow, and many positions offer flexibility, stability, and opportunities to work remotely. Since I value having a career that allows growth and adaptability, IT feels like a strong fit for me.

I also appreciate that there are many different paths within the field. Someone can start in an entry-level support role and eventually move into cybersecurity, cloud engineering, or systems administration. The variety makes it possible to continue growing and changing over time.

Most importantly, I enjoy learning new things and challenging myself. Although technology can sometimes feel intimidating, there is also something rewarding about understanding concepts that once seemed impossible.

Conclusion

Choosing to transition into IT after the military was not a decision I made overnight. It has involved uncertainty, a lot of studying, and stepping outside of my comfort zone. However, I believe the challenge is worth it because of the opportunities and growth the field offers.

Although I am still learning and figuring out exactly where I want to go in IT, I am excited for what comes next. For anyone considering a career change, especially after military service, my advice would be to stay open-minded, be patient with yourself, and remember that learning something new takes time.
